Rates are easing, but San Diego buyers aren't feeling it yet

Mortgage rates have come down since January. We went from 7.04% to 6.69% on the 30-year fixed, which on a $700K loan is about $160 less per month. That's real money. But San Diego home prices have climbed another 6% year over year, which more than wipes out the payment relief most buyers were hoping for.

The Fed has held rates steady through the first half of 2026. Futures markets are pricing in one or two cuts before year end. If that plays out, we could see 30-year rates approach the mid-6s by Q4. That would help at the margins, but with San Diego inventory still extremely tight, I don't expect prices to soften much even if rates drop another half point.

The most interesting thing I'm watching right now is the mortgage spread, which is the gap between the 30-year fixed and the 10-year Treasury. Historically that spread runs around 170 basis points. It's been running wider than normal for most of 2025 and 2026, which means there's room for mortgage rates to drop even without the Fed cutting if credit markets normalize.

For San Diego specifically: if you're waiting for rates to drop before buying, you may be waiting for something that doesn't help as much as you think. The city has a structural supply problem that rate cuts won't solve.

What I'm watching this month

Markets are pricing a 65% chance of a hold at the July meeting. Any surprise cut would immediately move mortgage rates. I'll be watching the language around inflation more than the rate decision itself.

Active listings in SD are still near historic lows. Any meaningful uptick in supply would be more impactful for affordability than a rate cut. I'm tracking this monthly via Redfin data.

The Fed's preferred inflation measure. A hot print pushes rate cut expectations further out and keeps mortgage rates elevated. A cool print could be the catalyst for rates to break below 6.5%.

The spread is the story: why mortgage rates aren't following the Treasury down

One of the most overlooked things in the mortgage market right now is the spread between the 30-year fixed rate and the 10-year Treasury yield. Historically that gap runs around 150 to 170 basis points. Right now it's sitting closer to 247 basis points. That difference matters more than most people realize.

What it means practically is that mortgage rates are being kept higher than Treasury yields would suggest. Even if the Fed cuts rates and the 10-year comes down, borrowers won't feel the full benefit until that spread normalizes. The spread is wide partly because of prepayment risk. When rates fall, homeowners refinance, which hurts mortgage-backed security investors. Credit market uncertainty is playing a role too.

The good news is that spreads do compress over time. If inflation continues cooling, we could see a double benefit: lower Treasury yields and a tightening spread. That combination could push the 30-year below 6% faster than most forecasters expect.

For anyone watching the mortgage market, and from my seat at Guild Mortgage I watch it closely, the spread is the number I check before the headline rate. It tells you more about where things are actually headed.

North Park vs El Cajon: a tale of two San Diego housing markets

When people talk about San Diego's housing affordability crisis, they usually point to one number: the countywide median price around $895,000. But that single figure hides an enormous amount of variation. North Park and El Cajon are both San Diego. They're also separated by $380,000 in median home prices and roughly $73,000 in annual income required to qualify for a mortgage.

North Park has become one of the most in-demand neighborhoods in the county. Walkability, restaurants, Balboa Park nearby. It checks every box for the young professional demographic. But a median price of $925,000 at today's rates puts monthly payments north of $6,000 before taxes and insurance. You need a household income approaching $180,000 to make that work on paper.

El Cajon tells a different story. At $545,000 median, monthly payments are around $2,900 on a 20% down purchase at today's rates. That's still not cheap. You need roughly $105,000 in household income. But it's within reach for dual-income households and puts homeownership on the table for people the rest of the county has priced out.

The point isn't that one neighborhood is better than the other. It's that San Diego's affordability crisis is really a dozen different crises depending on where you look. Anyone advising clients on real estate or financial planning in this market needs to understand that nuance.

Everyone in finance is talking about Nvidia, Microsoft, and the rest of the Magnificent 7. Those are great businesses. But the most interesting risk/reward setups are usually found where nobody is looking. Right now I think Agora (API) is one of the more compelling small-cap setups in the AI space.

Agora just completed its fifth consecutive quarter of GAAP profitability. That's genuinely rare for a growth software company at this stage. The stock trades at about 2.7x sales with a current ratio of 4.58, meaning they have nearly five times as much in current assets as current liabilities. The balance sheet is exceptionally strong for a company this size.

The pivot to Conversational AI Agents is well timed. Every company is trying to figure out how to deploy AI agents right now and Agora has the real-time communication infrastructure to actually build them. Revenue growth is only 13% year over year which isn't headline-grabbing, but the profitability trajectory and balance sheet discipline make it worth watching closely.

This is the kind of company that doesn't get written up until it's already moved 50%. Analyst consensus target is around $6.17 against a price near $4.20 at the time of writing. About 47% implied upside if they're right. I'm watching it.
